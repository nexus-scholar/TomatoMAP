# Gap and Motivation Note

## Motivation

Instance segmentation for greenhouse tomatoes is important because it supports downstream tasks such as phenotyping, maturity assessment, yield estimation, and robotic harvesting, yet it remains difficult in practice due to occlusion, scale variation, and dense clustering of fruits and flowers. Recent experimental results on the TomatoMAP segmentation setup show that even with modern YOLO segmentation models, performance is uneven across classes and clearly affected by data scale and class imbalance. [ppl-ai-file-upload.s3.amazonaws](https://ppl-ai-file-upload.s3.amazonaws.com/web/direct-files/attachments/78039023/53a60110-e791-4c9f-b542-0a9af551fcba/notebook010fccf34e-1.ipynb)

In my current TomatoMAP-based setup, the usable annotations are incomplete relative to the expected segmentation subset, which turns the problem into a realistic label-scarcity scenario rather than a standard fully supervised benchmark. This is not only a dataset inconvenience; it is a relevant research setting because real agricultural datasets are often expensive to annotate at the mask level, especially for small and overlapping objects. ## Evidence from current experiments

My notebook shows two practical regimes. A filtered subset built around the target classes contained 384 labeled images split into 256 training images and 128 validation images, with 1,175 validation instances, while the broader 10-class TomatoMAP segmentation evaluation used 243 validation images with 1,999 instances. [ppl-ai-file-upload.s3.amazonaws](https://ppl-ai-file-upload.s3.amazonaws.com/web/direct-files/attachments/78039023/53a60110-e791-4c9f-b542-0a9af551fcba/notebook010fccf34e-1.ipynb)

On the broader 10-class setup, a YOLO11l-seg model trained for 300 epochs at 1024 image size achieved validation mask mAP50 of 0.686 and mask mAP50-95 of 0.570. On the same validation set, box mAP50 reached 0.681 and box mAP50-95 reached 0.578, which suggests that segmentation is reasonably strong overall but still leaves meaningful room for improvement. ## Why the problem is still open

The notebook indicates strong class imbalance across the 10-class validation set. For example, the validation instances include 552 fully ripe objects, 296 unripe green tomato objects, and 246 objects in the 6mm class, but only 79 semi-ripe objects and 89 objects in the 2mm class. [ppl-ai-file-upload.s3.amazonaws](https://ppl-ai-file-upload.s3.amazonaws.com/web/direct-files/attachments/78039023/53a60110-e791-4c9f-b542-0a9af551fcba/notebook010fccf34e-1.ipynb)

Performance also varies strongly by class, which confirms that the task is not solved uniformly. In the YOLO11l-seg run, mask mAP50-95 reached 0.755 for the 12mm class and 0.749 for fully ripe fruits, but it dropped to 0.290 for the mini class, 0.263 for 2mm, 0.417 for nascent, and 0.493 for semi-ripe. These results imply that the main difficulty is not only achieving a good global metric, but achieving robust segmentation for small, visually ambiguous, and underrepresented categories. That pattern is exactly the kind of evidence that supports a paper framed around label efficiency, minority-class robustness, or supervision transfer rather than a generic “better backbone” paper. ## Research gap

The gap in my setting is the absence of a practical segmentation framework that remains effective when full mask annotations are incomplete and class coverage is uneven. My current results show that strong models can learn the dominant classes reasonably well, but the rare and small-object categories remain much weaker, which means the annotation problem is directly tied to the performance problem. [ppl-ai-file-upload.s3.amazonaws](https://ppl-ai-file-upload.s3.amazonaws.com/web/direct-files/attachments/78039023/53a60110-e791-4c9f-b542-0a9af551fcba/notebook010fccf34e-1.ipynb)

A second gap is methodological: the current experiments are still mainly standard supervised training runs, while the dataset situation naturally calls for methods that can exploit partially labeled data, unlabeled images, or weaker supervision. Because the available evidence already shows non-uniform class difficulty and limited labeled coverage, the most defensible next step is to study label-efficient or weakly supervised instance segmentation rather than only scaling supervised training. ## Proposed framing

Based on this evidence, the core motivation of the study can be stated as follows: **high-quality tomato instance segmentation remains limited by annotation scarcity and class imbalance, especially for small or rare developmental stages, even when modern segmentation models achieve reasonable overall mAP.** The contribution should therefore target how to improve segmentation under incomplete mask supervision, with special attention to minority and small-object classes. [ppl-ai-file-upload.s3.amazonaws](https://ppl-ai-file-upload.s3.amazonaws.com/web/direct-files/attachments/78039023/53a60110-e791-4c9f-b542-0a9af551fcba/notebook010fccf34e-1.ipynb)

A clear gap statement for the paper is: **existing supervised TomatoMAP experiments demonstrate usable overall performance, but they do not resolve the practical problem of missing mask labels and they still underperform on rare and small categories; therefore, a label-efficient segmentation strategy is needed.** This statement is directly supported by the split sizes, instance counts, and class-wise metric spread in the notebook. ## One-paragraph version

My preliminary TomatoMAP experiments show that current supervised segmentation models achieve reasonable overall performance, with YOLO11l-seg reaching mask mAP50 of 0.686 and mask mAP50-95 of 0.570 on a 10-class validation set of 243 images and 1,999 instances, but class-wise performance remains highly uneven because the data are imbalanced and some categories are small or rare. In addition, my usable labeled subset is limited, with a filtered setup of only 384 images split into 256 train and 128 validation images, which makes the project a realistic label-scarcity problem rather than a conventional full-supervision benchmark. These results motivate a study on label-efficient tomato instance segmentation, where the research gap is not simply better average accuracy, but better learning under incomplete mask supervision and better robustness on minority and small-object classes.
